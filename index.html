History of the USA

The United States of America, founded on July 4, 1776, emerged from thirteen British colonies seeking independence from Great Britain. The American War of Independence was fought from 1775 to 1783, culminating in the victory of the rebellious colonies and the formation of a new nation. In 1787, the United States Constitution was drafted, establishing a federal republic government and guaranteeing individual rights.

During the 19th and 20th centuries, the US expanded territorially westwards, faced a devastating civil war (1861-1865), and emerged as a global industrial and economic power. In the 20th century, it played crucial roles in the two world wars and the Cold War, consolidating its international influence.

The US is known for its cultural diversity, significant immigration, and role as a defender of individual rights and representative democracy. Today, it is one of the world's largest economies and continues to shape the global landscape in politics, economics, technology, and culture.

Geography of the USA

The geography of the United States is marked by vast diversity, influenced by its territorial extension, which makes it the fourth largest country in the world. Situated predominantly in North America, the United States shares borders with Canada to the north, the Atlantic Ocean to the east, the Gulf of Mexico and Mexico to the south, and the Pacific Ocean to the west. The country is home to a variety of ecosystems: temperate rainforests dominate the east and northwest coasts, while Florida is known for its swamps. The vast plains and the Mississippi-Missouri river system occupy the central region, followed by the imposing Rocky Mountains to the west of the plains. West of the Rockies, deserts and coastal zones complement the landscape, while temperate rainforests cover the northwest Pacific coast. Alaska adds an arctic region to the far north, while the volcanic islands of Hawaii further diversify the country's climate and geography.

Economy

The US economy is the largest in the world, with a nominal GDP estimated at over US$27.36 trillion in 2023, representing around 26.1% of the global economy. The country is highly developed, benefiting from abundant natural resources, a robust infrastructure, high levels of productivity and a well-educated workforce. Historically, the American economy has maintained stable GDP growth, low unemployment rates and high levels of research and investment, both nationally and internationally. Despite significant inequalities, the US economy remains a dominant force on the global economic stage.

Politics of the USA

The United States is a federal presidential republic. At the federal level, executive power is exercised by the President, who is elected by an electoral college, legislative power belongs to Congress, and judicial power belongs to the courts. Each state also elects its own governor (chief executive) and congress. There is also the possibility that, during the presidential election, the voters of a state may or may not approve bills on various issues.

Specific Traditions and Celebrations

Thanksgiving Day

Thanksgiving Day is one of the most iconic US holidays, celebrated on the fourth Thursday of November. It's a time to gather family and friends to express gratitude and share traditional meals such as roast turkey and pumpkin pie. The holiday also includes famous parades, such as the Macy's Thanksgiving Day Parade in New York, as well as religious and cultural events that unite communities across the country.

St.Patrick's Day

St. Patrick's Day, celebrated on March 17, is a cultural and religious festival that honors St. Patrick, the patron saint of Ireland. Originally a Christian holiday, it has become a popular celebration of Irish culture around the world, especially in the United States. The celebrations include parades, parties, traditional music, Celtic dances, green clothing and the consumption of typical Irish food and drink, such as the famous corned beef and cabbage. It's an opportunity for people from different backgrounds to get together and celebrate Irish heritage in a festive and joyful way.

From Blues to Pop

American music has been a pioneer in many genres, from the blues of B.B. King and Louis Armstrong to the rock of Elvis Presley and Bob Dylan. America's musical legacy continues with contemporary artists such as Beyoncé and Lady Gaga, who have achieved global fame and influenced the world music scene.

Patriotism

Patriotism is an intrinsic part of the American identity, visible in the ubiquitous national flags and the proudly sung national anthem. Sporting events, civic festivities, and even movies capture this sense of love for country and American values, uniting citizens in a sense of national identity.

Sports DNA

The United States is known for its vibrant and competitive sports culture. Baseball, considered the national sport, is closely followed by American soccer, ice hockey, and basketball. The NFL Super Bowl stands out as one of the most watched sporting events globally, reflecting the country's fascination with sports and the cultural impact of high-profile sporting events.

Independence Day (July 4th)

America's Independence Day, celebrated on July 4, commemorates the adoption of the Declaration of Independence in 1776. It is a national holiday marked by parades, fireworks, and barbecues throughout the country. Americans display the national flag and participate in events that highlight the values of freedom and self-determination that define the nation.

American customs

American breakfast: In the United States, breakfast is a substantial and valued meal, differing from many countries where it is lighter and quicker. Dishes such as bacon, eggs, pancakes, waffles and a variety of cereals are common, preparing Americans for a day full of activities.

Shopping: Consumerism is a hallmark of American culture, reflected in the appreciation of acquiring goods as part of the American dream. Large shopping malls and events such as Black Friday are emblematic of this socially engaged shopping culture.

Tipping: Tipping is an ingrained practice in the US, expected in restaurants, bars, cabs and other services. It usually ranges from 15% to 20% of the total bill, expressing gratitude for the service received.

Patriotism: Patriotism is widely celebrated in the US, visible on dates such as Independence Day and Memorial Day. National flags adorn homes and streets, while communities gather in parades and fireworks to pay homage to national history and values.

Sports: Sport is an essential part of American culture, with American soccer, basketball and baseball standing out. In addition to entertainment, sports are seen as educational tools that promote discipline and teamwork from an early age.

Halloween: Halloween is a colorful and lively festival in the USA, with elaborate decorations, creative costumes and the traditional trick-or-treating. It's a celebration that combines fun with artistic and cultural expression.

Fast Food: Fast food culture is prevalent in the US, offering convenience and a wide range of food options, adapted to the country's fast-paced and diverse lifestyle.

Independence and Mobility: Personal independence and mobility are core values in the US, encouraging Americans to seek opportunities in different parts of the country, whether for educational, work or personal reasons.

Best time to visit of the USA

The best times to visit the United States are spring (late March to late May) and fall (late September to late October). These are the seasons before and after summer, guaranteeing milder temperatures and fewer tourists in the most visited places. From east to west, the United States stretches for 2,680 km - encompassing 5 different climate patterns across its territory. Therefore, the best time to travel to the United States really depends on the specific place you want to visit. If you don't already have a destination in mind, spring and fall are considered the best times to visit the United States. From mid-March to the end of May, spring is synonymous with mild temperatures and beautiful natural landscapes in the national parks, forests and swamps of the southern states, such as California, Florida and Nevada. The northern states and cities take a while to get into the mood - but when they do, cities like New York, Washington D.C. and Chicago become popular urban destinations. Likewise, fall brings pleasant temperatures and stunning scenery to many parts of the country. For those who love outdoor activities such as hiking, biking and camping, these seasons are perfect for traveling.

Culinary

The cuisine of the United States reflects its cultural and regional diversity, influenced by various culinary traditions around the world. Here are 10 typical dishes that exemplify this variety:

Hamburger: An icon of American cuisine, adapted and popularized globally.

Waffle: Although not of American origin, it is widely consumed in the USA with a variety of accompaniments.

Pork ribs with barbecue sauce: A traditionally American dish, appreciated for its smoky flavor and varied sauces.

Hot dogs: A symbol of American street food, with its regional versions and favorite toppings.

Pancakes: Usually served for breakfast, they are thick and often accompanied by maple syrup, eggs and bacon.

Apple pie: A classic American dessert, with a generous filling and traditionally served with ice cream.

Macaroni and cheese: A simple and popular dish made with elbow macaroni and melted cheese.

Roast turkey in cranberry sauce: Associated with Thanksgiving, it is a central dish in many American family meals.

Doughnuts: Originating in the Netherlands but popularized in the US, these are fried doughs with a variety of toppings.

Cheesecake: A rich, creamy dessert with a cheese base and sweet topping, much appreciated in American cuisine.

Fauna

The fauna of the United States is diverse and includes unique species that are not found in other parts of the world. It includes approximately 432 species of mammals, more than 800 species of birds, more than 100,000 species of insects, 311 species of reptiles, 295 species of amphibians and 1154 species of fish. The USA is located in the Nearctic ecozone, characterized by a variety of wildlife ranging from deer and lynx to bears and eagles. National parks such as Yellowstone protect vast areas of natural habitats and are known for their wealth of wildlife, such as wolves and grizzly bears.

Turism

Accent

There are 11 American accents. North American accents vary significantly from region to region, reflecting the country's cultural and historical diversity. Here's a summary of the main accents:

Northeastern accent: Characterized by a rhythmic and distinctive pronunciation, with Irish and Italian influences in areas such as Boston and New York.

Southern accent: Marked by a slower pace and prolonged vowels, with notable variations between the Texas accent, the Georgia accent and other southern US states.

Midwestern accent: Known for being neutral and without many distinctive features, although it can vary between cities like Chicago and Minneapolis.

West Coast accent: Includes the Californian accent, which is often associated with the relaxed way of speaking and the use of local slang.

East Coast accent: Present in cities such as Philadelphia and Baltimore, characterized by a clearer and more distinctive accent.

New Orleans accent: Reflects the influence of French and Creole, with a soft and melodic pronunciation.

Appalachia accent: Found in the Appalachian mountains, it is known for its slow rhythm and rounded vowels.

Chicago accent: Distinguished by the use of "Chicago drawl" and variations in the pronunciation of vowels and consonants.

Alaskan accent: Influenced by indigenous and immigrant communities, with some regional variations due to the size of the state.

Hawaiian accent: Unique due to its isolated geographical location, with a mix of native and Asian influences.

Great Lakes accent: Found in the cities around the Great Lakes, characterized by a fast pace and distinctive vowel pronunciation.
